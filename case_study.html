Editorial Design for Health:

What Are Ultra-Processed Foods Doing to Our Health?

In recent decades, the modern diet has shifted dramatically toward ultra-processed foods, products manufactured through multiple industrial processes from ingredients rarely found in a home kitchen. In the United States, these foods account for more than half of all daily calories consumed. I was drawn to this topic through personal experience and a growing curiosity about how systems like food production and convenience culture shape health in ways most people never stop to question. The result was a mock editorial article designed for Healthy Living magazine.

Research Phase

Research confirmed what early data suggested: ultra-processed food consumption is widespread across all demographics, with particularly high rates among younger populations, and is consistently linked to serious adverse health outcomes across large-scale epidemiological studies. Faced with the challenge of translating nutritional science into an accessible editorial format, I focused on building an article that could explain what ultra-processed foods actually are, put their prevalence into perspective, and offer readers a clear path toward more informed choices without overwhelming them.

Visual Language

My initial concept was to use color to draw a distinction between ultra-processed and whole foods. As the project developed, I realized the more powerful move was to reframe how readers see familiar products. These are foods people buy without a second thought, and orange plays a direct role in why. Research into food packaging shows that orange stimulates impulse buying and is deeply associated with convenience snacks and affordable, ready-to-eat products. Cheetos, Fanta, cheap fast food packaging. Orange is already the color of ultra-processed food culture. Rather than fight that association, I leaned into it. Combined with dark grey through a threshold filter and gradient map applied to every product image, the palette turns familiar comfort foods into something that reads more like a warning label than a grocery store display. Typography followed the same logic, with Playfair Display Italic for headlines bringing editorial authority, while Source Sans Pro handles body and caption text with neutrality. I also replicated Healthy Living's grid directly, tracing and measuring actual pages to build an accurate InDesign grid, and carried over small details like page numbers, section labels, and margin text so the article would feel native to the publication.

Draft Review

The first draft surfaced a clear problem: each spread was making its own decisions without enough reference to the others. The cover had energy but no system. The interior spreads had structure but no personality. In response, I applied the threshold filter and gradient map treatment consistently across all food imagery, which immediately created a shared visual identity across all three spreads. I also locked in a consistent typographic hierarchy and reintegrated the small editorial details borrowed from Healthy Living, including margin annotations, section labels, and page numbers, so that every spread felt like part of the same publication.

Complexity + Insight

This project required navigating complexity at several levels at once: the subject matter, the medium, and the audience. Ultra-processed food is a topic most readers have a personal relationship with, which meant the design couldn't be alarmist or preachy. It needed to inform without lecturing. The editorial format added its own constraints, requiring every layout decision to serve both the argument of the article and the conventions of the magazine. And throughout all of that, the visual system had to hold together across three spreads with very different content demands. The solution was a small set of rules applied consistently: the threshold filter and gradient map on every image, a locked typographic hierarchy, and the editorial details borrowed from Healthy Living carried through every spread. Once those rules were in place, the article had room to vary without losing coherence. What this project reinforced is that the right constraints, set early, do most of the work. When the rules are specific, they bring coherence to a complex topic without having to solve it the same way twice.
